Medicare for All?
The Politics

While running for a position in the federal government is an unrealistic expectation of anyone, after turning 18 almost every American can register to vote. You can vote for candidates for the Senate, House of Representatives, or President that support Medicare-for-All.

In order for Medicare-for-All to pass, there would need to be a simple majority in the Senate and House of Representatives that support it. Then, there would need to be a President that would not veto the bill however, if there was ⅔ majority in the Senate then the bill would be veto-proof. Currently, both Massachusetts senators support Medicare-for-All.

Who Represents You

For most people who live in the Middlesex area, such as Dracut or Lowell, Lori Trahan is your representative. She supports expanding the Affordable Care Act, a healthcare system that is still quite expensive for the impoverished. You also could face penalties for not having insurance, which many Americans are unable to afford.

‘Romneycare,’ which the Affordable Care Act is based on, did not improve control of diabetes among low-income groups. A more comprehensive plan, like Medicare-for-All, would benefit lower-income groups the most, and would not penalize them.

Even though a single-payer healthcare system like Medicare-for-All would significantly help end the diabetes epidemic, other issues still need to be addressed. Low-income communities stuck with lower quality education need to receive more funding. Having a worse education leads to a higher chance of being poor, creating a reinforcing poverty loop.

Social programs such as food stamps would help support lower-income communities as well. Middle-class income stagnation should be ended and should rise along with productivity. If middle-class income stagnation continues, then a ‘middle-class squeeze’ is created, which would cause the middle-class to become the lower-class, only further contributing to the emerging diabetes epidemic.
